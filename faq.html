Frequently Asked Questions

1. Are corals plants or animals? ▼

Corals are actually marine animals! They are related to jellyfish and sea anemones and are made up of tiny, tentacled creatures called polyps.

2. How do corals get their bright colors? ▼

Their color comes from microscopic algae called zooxanthellae that live inside the coral's tissues. The algae provide food for the coral through photosynthesis.

3. What is coral bleaching? ▼

When the water gets too warm or polluted, corals become stressed and expel the colorful algae living inside them. This leaves them looking completely white, or "bleached," and makes them very vulnerable.

4. Can a coral reef recover from bleaching? ▼

Yes, if water temperatures return to normal quickly enough, the algae can return, and the coral can survive. However, severe or prolonged bleaching often leads to coral death.

5. Why are coral reefs called the "rainforests of the sea"? ▼

Because of their incredible biodiversity! Though they cover less than 1% of the ocean floor, they support around 25% of all marine life.

6. How fast do coral reefs grow? ▼

Very slowly! Depending on the species, corals grow anywhere from 0.3 to 10 centimeters per year. A large reef takes thousands of years to form.

7. What is the largest coral reef system in the world? ▼

The Great Barrier Reef in Australia. It is so massive that it stretches over 1,400 miles and can even be seen from space!

8. What do corals eat? ▼

Most of their nutrients come from their resident algae. However, they also hunt at night by extending their tentacles to catch microscopic plankton floating by.

9. How do coral reefs protect our coastlines? ▼

Reefs act as natural barriers. They absorb the energy of waves during storms and hurricanes, preventing coastal erosion and protecting coastal communities.

10. Where are most coral reefs located? ▼

Most reef-building corals are found in shallow, clear, tropical, and subtropical waters where sunlight can penetrate to feed their algae.

11. Are coral reefs in danger? ▼

Yes. They face severe threats from climate change, ocean acidification, overfishing, and coastal pollution. Scientists estimate we have already lost 50% of the world's reefs.

12. What is ocean acidification? ▼

As the ocean absorbs excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, the water becomes more acidic. This makes it much harder for corals to build their hard calcium carbonate skeletons.

13. How do corals reproduce? ▼

They can reproduce asexually by budding (cloning themselves) to grow the colony, or sexually through "mass spawning," where millions of eggs and sperm are released into the water at the same time.

14. Is it safe to touch a coral reef? ▼

No! Touching corals can scrape off their protective mucus layer, making them susceptible to disease. Plus, some corals (like fire coral) can give you a painful sting.

15. How can I help protect coral reefs? ▼

You can help by reducing your carbon footprint, using "reef-safe" mineral sunscreens, eating sustainable seafood, and never touching or stepping on reefs when snorkeling.
